01 · Background

AbbVie's field teams run on data. The tool they had wasn't making that easy.

AbbVie is a global biopharmaceutical company known for Humira, Imbruvica, and Rinvoq across immunology, oncology, and rare disease. Their field teams — CSS specialists, area managers, and home office staff — use data daily to manage accounts, track patient reimbursement, and plan quarterly strategy.

CompletePro was the internal Power BI dashboard built to support all of that. It had the data, but most users were doing their actual analysis in Excel. That's where I came in.

02 · The problem

Too much information. Not organised in a way that matched how people worked.

Where most real analysis was actually happening

CompletePro had everything the team needed on paper. The problem was that it was structured around data categories, not around how people actually worked day to day.

CSS field specialists — the most frequent users — were exporting data to Excel to do their real analysis because the dashboard wasn't organised in a way that matched their workflow. Area managers were manually resetting the same filters at the start of every session. Home office users were navigating tabs that weren't built for their needs.

The tool had been built around what data was available, not what users needed to do with it. Everything was given equal weight, so nothing stood out, and the tool felt overwhelming to work in.

04 · Research

9+ interviews. Three things came up in every conversation.

Trust in the tool was low
Not because the data was wrong, but because the interface made it hard to feel confident about what you were looking at. Too many columns, unclear labels, and visual noise meant users second-guessed the numbers rather than acting on them.
The tool's structure didn't match how people thought about their work
CompletePro was organised by data type — tabs for management, performance, pharmacy, PA, BV. But users thought in terms of accounts and account journeys. The two didn't line up, so people built workarounds.
No way to work as a team inside it
Field specialists handed off insights to area managers through email and separate decks. CompletePro was an individual analysis tool but the work it supported was collaborative. Every team handoff meant leaving the product.
Excel was the real workaround
The clearest signal that something was wrong: CSS field specialists weren't using CompletePro for analysis. They were using it to pull data, then doing their actual thinking in Excel. When users build workarounds that consistently bypass your tool, the tool has a problem — not the users.
05 · Audit findings

I audited every tab in CompletePro. The problems fell into four areas.

Audit documentation / annotated screenshots
Drop audit images here before publishing
Navigation & orientation
The landing page looked more like a user manual than a dashboard. Long horizontal layout, no visual hierarchy, no breadcrumbs. Icons weren't labelled. Filters had similar-looking icons that did different things.
Information overload
The Performance tab had so many columns users couldn't work with it — they scrolled right, gave up, and opened Excel. No way to hide columns, prioritise information, or customise the view to what mattered.
Filter friction
Filters had no memory between sessions — users started from scratch every time. No defaults per role. Unclear whether fields accepted single or multiple selections. Filters didn't carry across related tabs.
Redundancy & inconsistency
The same sub-tab appeared in two different places. Low-traffic tabs had the same visual weight as heavily used ones. Button colours were inconsistent. KPI cards didn't show which time period they covered.
06 · The solution

From organised by data type, to organised by how people actually work.

The redesign was built around one change in thinking. Instead of tabs named after data categories, the structure was rebuilt around the account journey — what a CSS specialist or area manager actually needs to do when they open the tool.

1
Planning module built around N+Rc
A new planning module built around New accounts, Reactivated accounts, and Carryover. For the first time, quarterly planning could happen inside CompletePro rather than in a separate spreadsheet.
2
Reorganised information hierarchy
Each tab restructured so the most used information comes first. KPI cards given clear time context. Performance tab columns categorised and made collapsible — users can focus without losing access to everything else.
3
Role-aware defaults
Default filters set per user role. A CSS field specialist sees their region and accounts pre-selected. An area manager sees their team. The repetitive setup at the start of every session is gone.
4
Cleaner UI and collaboration added
Gradient backgrounds replaced with solid colours. Icons labelled. Consistent button hierarchy. A report log and download capability added so field specialists can create and share reports without leaving CompletePro.

08 · Use cases

Three planning modes. One tool that had to work for all of them.

The use cases were developed based on how different user groups actually plan their work — strategically across a quarter, operationally week to week, and tactically in the moment before a visit.

CSS Field
Area Manager
Home Office
Weekly performance review
A CSS field user logs in to evaluate their weekly performance. They examine how many accounts they covered in the past week, identify the ones they were unable to address, and review the status of those accounts and their Le3 and G3 percentage splits to decide on next steps and prioritise follow-ups.
Regional performance overview
An area manager reviews quarterly team performance through geographical representation, giving a visual overview across different areas. They gain insight into how other regions have performed, allowing them to compare and contrast their team's results with those of other regions.
Coaching report analysis
At the end of the quarter, a home office user reviews the coaching report to analyse the number of field travels conducted by area managers. After completing the overview, they share the report with the director for further analysis.
Monthly performance report
A home office user generates a monthly report that includes all primary KPIs to evaluate employee performance. This comprehensive report allows them to assess how employees are meeting targets, track progress over time, and identify areas for improvement or further training.
Account status change alerts
A CSS field user receives a notification when one of their account's status changes from active to inactive during the quarter. This alert helps the user stay informed and take prompt action. The notification feature can be modified as per Area Managers' needs as well.

Danger zone prioritisation
A CSS field employee begins their week by reviewing the number of accounts that are alarming or in the danger zone. This helps them schedule their week's visit planner. The employee prints the list and prioritises in-person visits with these accounts over virtual only users.
Quarterly KPI review
An area manager logs in to monitor their team's performance over the last quarter by exploring primary KPIs — total N+Rc, carryover with L3 and G3 split, total number of calls, and total live calls. By assessing these metrics, they evaluate team performance and prioritise follow-ups accordingly.
Carryover account management
An area manager can review carryover accounts by toggling between Le3 and G3 accounts. This allows informed decisions on further actions, prioritising attention to Le3 accounts, which may require more focus compared to G3 accounts.
Account status change tracking
An individual view provides detailed insights into the reasons behind changes in account status, such as transitioning from active to inactive. This includes information on who initiated the status change and why.

Pre-visit account deep-dive
A CSS field user navigates the dashboard to deep-dive into individual accounts and assess their current status. They identify users they have already visited and pinpoint the ones they should prioritise for their next visit. This helps plan follow-up actions and manage workload more effectively.
Call activity analysis
An area manager conducts an in-depth analysis of individual accounts by reviewing call activities, divided into in-person, virtual, and telephonic calls. This allows the manager to understand the proportion of in-person calls made by each account during the day or week.
Account demographics access
When tracking account activity, CSS users find it convenient to read account demographics such as names and email addresses, regardless of list length. This allows them to efficiently leverage the information by copying email addresses for further processing if needed.
iRep and FAS detail view
An account activity deep-dive view provides comprehensive insights into iRep details, including a list of all sales representatives, with differentiation between FAS. This information assists the CSS team during their follow-up emails.

10 · Constraints & learnings

What shaped the work. What I took away from it.

Constraints
The tool runs on Power BI — every design decision had to work within Power BI's layout and component system, which limits what's possible at the UI level.
Different user groups needed different things from the same data. Role-based defaults helped, but they were a workaround for a deeper personalisation gap that Power BI couldn't fully solve.
Some tabs had low usage because of underlying data quality issues, not just design problems. Improving the surface didn't fix what was underneath.
Learnings
The audit was the most important part of this project. Without it, I would have been redesigning symptoms rather than causes. The real problem wasn't any single screen — it was that the tool had been built for the people who commissioned it, not the people who used it every day.
Small daily friction adds up. Resetting filters every morning. Exporting to Excel every week. None of these feel like a big deal alone. But across a whole field team, every day, fixing them has a real impact on how much time people have to do actual work.
