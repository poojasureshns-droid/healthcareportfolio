01 · Background

Merck runs on a mission — give cancer patients more time.

More quality of life. More access to treatment. More time. To make that happen, the people inside Merck need to be able to do their jobs well — analyse data precisely, plan strategically, and make decisions quickly.

When the reporting tools slow them down, everything downstream suffers. That's where this project began.

02 · The problem

Too many reports. None of them telling the same story.

20+
External vendors feeding data into Merck's reporting
0
Consistent KPI definitions across those vendors
↑ hrs
Time spent fixing data instead of analysing it

The team was working with data from 20+ vendors. Each vendor had their own way of defining KPIs, labelling metrics, and structuring reports. When the team needed to understand market performance, they had to open multiple reports, manually compare numbers, and figure out why the same metric showed different values in different places.

Over time, people created their own workaround reports. But those reports were built on different logic, so they'd produce different conclusions. The same question — "how is Keytruda performing in NSCLC this quarter?" — could get five different answers depending on who you asked.

The result: nobody fully trusted the data. And when you can't trust your data, you spend more time validating it than using it.

"It is quite frustrating when you spend more time wrestling with spreadsheets than actually analyzing the data."

04 · What the audit told us

Before designing, I audited the existing GAIN dashboard. Three problems stood out.

Navigation & orientation
Users couldn't easily tell where they were or how to get somewhere else. The menu only went home. No breadcrumbs. Filter hierarchies weren't communicated visually, so users had to guess what was selected and what wasn't.
Information density
The data table was too short to read without constant scrolling. Active and inactive tabs looked too similar. Icons had no labels. Small things individually — but together they created real friction for people who spent hours in this tool every day.
No collaboration built in
The dashboard was built for one person analysing alone. There was no way to make a report, share it, or discuss it inside the product. Every team handoff meant leaving the tool — which is exactly why people kept building their own workaround reports.
05 · The solution

GAIN — one platform, built on four things the team actually needed.

The goal was simple: one source of truth, accessible to everyone, with consistent data that people could actually trust. We built it around four pillars.

1
Data standardisation at ingestion
All vendor data passes through a standardisation layer before anyone sees it. Different KPI names, definitions, and formats get resolved automatically. What reaches the user is clean and consistent — no manual reconciliation needed.
2
Multi-dimensional data analysis
KPIs can be broken down and compared across multiple dimensions at once — region, regimen, tumor type, line of therapy, time period. Users can look at the same data through multiple lenses without switching reports.
3
Gen AI alerts & auto-analysis
The system monitors performance automatically. If Keytruda's NPS drops in a specific segment, the platform flags it. If a competitor campaign picks up traction, the team gets an alert. You don't have to go looking — the insight comes to you.
4
Shared report log
A report log visible to the whole team. Reports are created, shared, and discussed inside the platform. Everyone's working from the same data, the same document, and the same conclusions — no more parallel report versions floating around in email.

07 · Use cases

Two real scenarios the design had to solve for.

Use Case 01
There's a dip in new patient starts — why?

The Director spots a drop in NPS (new patient starts) in the NSCLC tumor type segment. He needs to understand how bad it is and what's causing it. He asks the Senior Specialist to dig into the data and come back with a clear picture.

What the team needed to be able to do
Filter the dashboard to the NSCLC segment and see the numbers clearly
Drill into the KPIs — regimen, region, line of therapy — to find where the dip is coming from
Build a report with the findings and share it with the Director inside the platform
Discuss next steps together without switching to a separate tool

Use Case 02
A competitor launches a campaign 3 days before us — now what?

Keytruda is running an HCP awareness campaign for Advanced RCC. Opdivo launches something similar three days earlier. Historically, Opdivo campaigns have hurt Keytruda's NPS growth. The Director needs to watch both campaigns in real time and respond fast enough to protect Keytruda's performance.

What the team needed to be able to do
Monitor NPS for both Keytruda and Opdivo in the same view, in real time
Compare trends across the same time window to catch early signals of impact
Get alerted automatically if Keytruda's numbers start to move in the wrong direction
Brief the team and align on a response — all without leaving the platform

09 · Constraints & learnings

What shaped the work. What I took away from it.

Constraints
Vendor data couldn't be restructured at source — standardisation had to happen at ingestion, which shaped the entire data architecture.
Regulatory requirements around data governance determined what could be surfaced automatically and what required user-initiated access.
The platform had to work for both senior directors needing summary views and specialists needing granular drill-downs — without feeling like a compromise for either.
Learnings
The problem wasn't a UI problem — it was a trust problem. Reports were being ignored not because they were hard to use, but because nobody was confident the numbers were right.
Collaboration can't be added on after the fact. The absence of a shared report log was the reason the fragmentation kept happening — people built workarounds because there was no shared version.
Designing communication into the core of the product — not as a feature — changed how the team worked.
